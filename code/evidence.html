Evidence Snapshot (Contextual Data)

Because this capstone did not collect primary data, the evidence snapshot anchors the problem using publicly available corrections workforce datasets and peer-reviewed studies on stress, trauma exposure, and burnout. These sources help leadership understand baseline risk patterns and define what outcomes an intervention like TIC should target.

Public datasets you can cite

  • ICPSR 37329: Effects of Stress Among Correctional Officers, U.S., 2017–2018.
  • ICPSR 38442: Defining Impact of Stress and Traumatic Events on Corrections Officers, Oregon, 2018–2020.

These datasets support descriptive and correlational analyses (e.g., stress exposure, organizational outcomes, well-being indicators). They do not directly test TIC training in juvenile justice, but they strengthen the rationale and measurement strategy.

Peer-reviewed anchors

  • Jail officer PTSD and burnout research highlights the high burden of trauma exposure in correctional workforces.
  • Juvenile justice staff perceptions of TIC have been linked to burnout and turnover intentions.

See the References tab for APA citations and links.

Consulting translation: Use this section to justify an intervention budget. Burnout can be reframed as a measurable operational risk (retention, overtime, safety climate, and service disruptions).

What we can responsibly claim from existing evidence

  • Corrections and justice professionals face elevated occupational stress and trauma exposure.
  • Stress and trauma exposure are associated with negative well-being and organizational outcomes.
  • In juvenile justice settings, perceptions of TIC are associated with burnout and turnover intentions.
