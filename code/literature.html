Literature Review (Ivy-style synthesis)

The literature converges on a consistent pattern: when professionals work in environments characterized by repeated exposure to trauma and chronic job demands, burnout risk increases—especially emotional exhaustion and depersonalization. Trauma-informed care (TIC) enters as a systems-level framework that targets organizational climate, shared language, and psychological safety.

Theme 1: Secondary traumatic stress and compassion fatigue

  • Professionals working with traumatized populations may develop secondary traumatic stress (STS), which can intensify burnout risk.
  • Foundational work on compassion fatigue provides the conceptual base for organizational prevention strategies.

Theme 2: Burnout as an occupational health outcome

  • Burnout is commonly conceptualized across emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and personal accomplishment.
  • Occupational health psychology emphasizes aligning interventions with organizational demands, support structures, and leadership behavior.

Theme 3: TIC as an organizational intervention

  • TIC models emphasize safety, trustworthiness, empowerment, collaboration, and cultural responsiveness.
  • In juvenile justice staff populations, perceived TIC has been empirically linked with burnout and turnover intentions.
  • Qualitative work in correctional environments highlights implementation realities—training must be reinforced through supervision and climate.
Consulting move: TIC is strongest when treated as a change-management program (training + coaching + policy alignment), not a one-time workshop.
